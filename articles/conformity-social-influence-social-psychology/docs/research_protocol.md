# Research Protocol: Conformity and Social Influence

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on conformity, social influence, norm formation, majority pressure, informational uncertainty, normative pressure, visible dissent, minority influence, digital social proof, and institutional conformity.

It supports Asch-type line-judgment paradigms, Sherif-style ambiguous judgment tasks, vignette experiments, organizational surveys, platform experiments, network diffusion simulations, and institutional decision-process research.

## Core constructs

### Ambiguity

The degree to which a correct judgment is uncertain, underdetermined, difficult, or open to interpretation.

### Normative pressure

The perceived social cost of disagreement and the perceived benefit of acceptance, approval, belonging, or status.

### Informational influence

The extent to which others are treated as sources of evidence about reality, especially under uncertainty.

### Unanimity

The perceived absence of dissent within the influencing group.

### Visible dissent

The presence of an ally, dissenter, minority voice, or alternative interpretation that breaks unanimity.

### Cohesion

The psychological importance of belonging to the group.

### Status strength

The authority, prestige, expertise, rank, or reputational weight of the source of influence.

### Public response

Whether the participant’s judgment is observable by others.

### Minority consistency

The degree to which a dissenting minority maintains a stable position over time.

### Digital social proof

Visible signals such as likes, shares, ratings, reposts, trending indicators, follower counts, and algorithmic ranking.

### Institutional conformity

Alignment with organizational norms, leadership expectations, professional culture, or bureaucratic routines.

## Recommended designs

1. Ambiguous versus clear judgment condition.
2. Unanimous majority versus broken-unanimity condition.
3. Public response versus private response condition.
4. High-cohesion versus low-cohesion group condition.
5. High-status majority versus equal-status majority.
6. Visible ally / dissent condition.
7. Consistent minority versus inconsistent minority.
8. Digital social-proof manipulation.
9. Algorithmic ranking / popularity cue experiment.
10. Organizational conformity and psychological-safety field survey.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish public compliance from private acceptance.
- Measure confidence, not only choice.
- Measure response time, hesitation, and post-trial explanation where possible.
- Separate normative pressure from informational uncertainty.
- Measure visible dissent and perceived unanimity.
- Measure status strength and group identification separately.
- Treat cultural differences carefully; do not reduce them to stereotypes.
- Avoid treating conformity as inherently pathological.
- Distinguish coordination, learning, compliance, internalization, and coercive silence.

## Ethics

Conformity studies can involve deception, social pressure, public embarrassment, or manipulation of perceived group consensus. Researchers should minimize distress, debrief carefully, protect participant withdrawal, and avoid stigmatizing participants who conform.
