# Research Protocol: The Bystander Effect

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on the bystander effect: the decreased likelihood or increased latency of helping behavior when other observers are present.

It supports laboratory experiments, emergency-vignette studies, field simulations, online harassment and cyberbullying studies, organizational reporting studies, public-safety research, and institutional intervention training.

## Core constructs

### Bystander count

Number of actual or perceived witnesses to the event.

### Perceived responsibility

Subjective sense that one personally should act.

### Diffusion of responsibility

Reduction in perceived personal responsibility as responsibility appears distributed across others.

### Emergency clarity

Degree to which the situation is clearly understood as requiring intervention.

### Pluralistic ignorance

Misreading the calm or passive behavior of others as evidence that intervention is unnecessary.

### Evaluation apprehension

Fear of embarrassment, social judgment, overreaction, or doing the wrong thing.

### Victim identifiability

Degree to which the person needing help is personally identifiable, socially close, or humanly vivid.

### Shared identity

Perceived common identity between bystander and victim, or between bystanders and a helping norm.

### Direct responsibility assignment

A cue that makes one bystander specifically responsible, such as "You in the blue jacket, call 911."

### Perceived competence

Belief that one knows how to help.

### Cost of intervention

Perceived physical, social, reputational, legal, or practical cost of helping.

### Intervention norm salience

Visibility of a norm that helping is expected, legitimate, and supported.

### Online bystander context

Digital setting involving harassment, cyberbullying, misinformation, self-harm risk, or abusive behavior.

## Recommended designs

1. Alone versus multiple-bystander emergency paradigm.
2. Perceived bystander-count manipulation.
3. Emergency clarity manipulation.
4. Direct responsibility assignment experiment.
5. Evaluation apprehension manipulation.
6. Shared identity manipulation.
7. Victim identifiability manipulation.
8. Online cyberbullying bystander intervention experiment.
9. Organizational reporting/whistleblowing vignette design.
10. Training intervention with pre/post measurement.

## Measurement cautions

- Measure perceived bystander count, not only objective count.
- Distinguish helping intention from actual intervention.
- Measure latency as well as intervention.
- Distinguish safe direct intervention from indirect intervention.
- Include emergency clarity and perceived competence.
- Measure social-evaluation concerns.
- Measure shared identity and victim identifiability.
- In online studies, measure anonymity, platform norms, moderation visibility, and perceived audience size.
- Avoid treating nonintervention as indifference without measuring fear, ambiguity, competence, and risk.

## Ethics

Bystander-effect research often involves distressing emergency scenarios, harassment scenarios, or simulated victimization. Researchers should minimize distress, avoid deception unless necessary and approved, provide debriefing, protect participants and confederates, and avoid creating real risk.
