# Measurement Notes

## Five-step intervention model

A bystander must generally:

1. Notice the event.
2. Interpret the event as requiring help.
3. Assume personal responsibility.
4. Know what form of assistance to provide.
5. Implement the action.

Each step is a potential point of failure.

## Diffusion of responsibility

A simple diffusion model:

Responsibility_i = 1 / perceived_bystander_count

A more realistic model uses responsibility assignment and shared identity:

Responsibility_i = f(
  perceived_bystander_count,
  direct_assignment,
  shared_identity,
  leadership_cue,
  intervention_norm_salience
)

## Helping probability

Helping probability should include:

- felt responsibility
- emergency clarity
- victim identifiability
- shared identity
- perceived competence
- intervention norms
- direct assignment

and should decrease with:

- evaluation apprehension
- intervention cost
- pluralistic ignorance
- uncertainty
- perceived risk

## Online settings

Online bystander behavior should distinguish:

- visible audience size
- anonymity/pseudonymity
- moderation visibility
- norm salience
- platform traceability
- relationship to victim
- risk of retaliation
- ease of indirect intervention

## Recommended reliability checks

- Responsibility scale reliability
- Evaluation apprehension scale reliability
- Emergency clarity scale reliability
- Perceived competence scale reliability
- Shared identity scale reliability
- Online moderation/norm salience scale reliability

## Recommended validity checks

- Verify bystander-count manipulation affected perceived responsibility.
- Verify emergency-clarity manipulation changed interpretation of the situation.
- Verify direct assignment increased felt responsibility.
- Test whether responsibility mediates bystander-count effects.
- Test whether shared identity attenuates the bystander effect.
- Test whether intervention training increases perceived competence.
