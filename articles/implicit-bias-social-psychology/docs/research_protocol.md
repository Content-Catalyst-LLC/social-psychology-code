# Research Protocol: Implicit Bias in Social Psychology

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on implicit bias, automatic associations, social categorization, IAT-style response latency, explicit attitudes, behavioral outcomes, intervention design, and institutional disparity.

It supports IAT-style experiments, evaluative priming studies, vignette studies, audit-style decision tasks, healthcare/education/workplace judgment studies, randomized interventions, repeated-measure designs, and institutional simulations.

## Core constructs

### Implicit association

A measured association between a social category and an evaluative or semantic attribute, often inferred from response-latency differences.

### Explicit attitude

A self-reported, reflective attitude or belief toward a social group or category.

### Congruent and incongruent blocks

Task blocks in which social categories and attributes are paired in ways expected to be relatively easier or harder given associative structure.

### D-score

A standardized latency-difference score commonly used in IAT-style analysis.

### Cognitive load

A condition that may increase reliance on automatic processing by limiting reflective control.

### Accountability

A condition that may reduce biased judgment by increasing the expectation of justification.

### Time pressure

A condition that may increase the influence of automatic associations.

### Counter-stereotypical exposure

Exposure to examples that disrupt repeated category-attribute pairings.

### Structured decision support

Procedures that reduce reliance on unstructured discretion, such as rubrics, checklists, blind review, decision logs, or calibration.

### Institutional disparity

Cumulative unequal outcomes produced by repeated small decision differences across settings.

## Recommended designs

1. IAT-style latency task with congruent/incongruent blocks.
2. Explicit-attitude survey paired with implicit measure.
3. Judgment task under time pressure versus reflective review.
4. Cognitive-load manipulation.
5. Accountability and justification manipulation.
6. Counter-stereotypical exposure intervention.
7. Perspective-taking or individuation intervention.
8. Structured-decision-support intervention.
9. Follow-up measurement to test durability.
10. Institutional simulation of repeated small disparities.

## Measurement cautions

- Do not treat a single IAT score as a moral diagnosis of a person.
- Distinguish person-level prediction from aggregate association.
- Separate implicit associations, explicit attitudes, stereotypes, cultural knowledge, and behavior.
- Report reliability, exclusion criteria, scoring procedure, and latency trimming.
- Model trial-level response time where possible.
- Avoid treating bias training as sufficient without structural safeguards.
- Distinguish reducing implicit associations from reducing biased outcomes.
- Use preregistered analysis plans where possible.

## Ethics

Implicit-bias research can involve sensitive social categories and personally unsettling feedback. Researchers should avoid shaming participants, protect privacy, debrief carefully, and explain that implicit measures are probabilistic research tools, not definitive character judgments.
