# Research Protocol: Deindividuation

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on deindividuation: conditions in which anonymity, group immersion, lowered accountability, and shifts in identity salience change behavioral regulation.

The protocol supports laboratory anonymity experiments, crowd-behavior designs, online disinhibition studies, SIDE-model tests, platform-governance studies, organizational role-immersion studies, and political/crowd behavior research.

## Core constructs

### Anonymity

Degree to which the actor is personally unidentifiable to others.

### Identifiability

Degree to which personal identity, name, face, profile, or traceability is visible.

### Self-awareness

Attention to personal standards, internal monitoring, self-reflection, and personal accountability.

### Accountability

Expectation that one's behavior can be attributed, judged, sanctioned, or evaluated.

### Group identity salience

Degree to which a shared social identity is psychologically active.

### Group norm valence

Whether the salient group norm is prosocial, antisocial, punitive, celebratory, cooperative, exclusionary, or neutral.

### Norm clarity

How clear, visible, and behaviorally actionable the group norm is.

### Norm congruence

Degree to which behavior aligns with the salient group norm.

### Crowd immersion

Subjective feeling of being absorbed into a group, crowd, collective, platform community, or role system.

### Emotional contagion

Degree to which affect is amplified through group interaction.

### Responsibility diffusion

Degree to which responsibility feels distributed across the group rather than personally located.

### Moral disengagement

Cognitive rationalization of harmful behavior through displacement, diffusion, euphemism, blame, or dehumanization.

### Platform moderation visibility

Degree to which digital rules, enforcement, and accountability are visible in online settings.

## Recommended designs

1. Identified versus anonymous condition.
2. Anonymous with prosocial versus antisocial norm cues.
3. Self-awareness manipulation using mirrors, cameras, name tags, or private reflection prompts.
4. Group identity salience manipulation.
5. SIDE design: anonymity crossed with group identity salience and norm valence.
6. Online pseudonymity and moderation visibility experiment.
7. Crowd immersion and emotional arousal field survey.
8. Role-immersion experiment in organizational or institutional context.
9. Multi-level design with individuals nested in groups or online communities.
10. Cross-platform comparison of anonymity, moderation, and norm enforcement.

## Measurement cautions

- Do not treat anonymity as automatically antisocial.
- Measure salient group norms and norm valence.
- Distinguish personal identity loss from social identity salience.
- Measure accountability separately from identifiability.
- Measure self-awareness directly rather than inferring it from anonymity.
- Include prosocial and antisocial outcomes.
- Avoid labeling crowd behavior irrational without norm and identity evidence.
- Include power, institutional role, leadership, and platform design when relevant.
- Interpret online anonymity through visibility, traceability, group identity, moderation, and norm structure.

## Ethics

Deindividuation studies can involve anonymity, aggression tasks, deception, harmful language, crowd cues, platform behavior, and sensitive group identity contexts. Researchers should minimize harm, avoid inducing actual harassment, debrief participants, protect privacy, and avoid stigmatizing groups or crowds as inherently irrational.
