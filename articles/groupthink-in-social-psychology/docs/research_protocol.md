# Research Protocol: Groupthink in Social Psychology

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on groupthink: the failure of collective decision-making that occurs when pressure for agreement, cohesion, directive leadership, stress, and informational insulation suppress dissent and weaken critical evaluation.

It supports laboratory group studies, organizational decision research, leadership-team analysis, crisis-decision simulations, public-policy case coding, board and committee research, expert-panel evaluation, and institutional-governance audits.

## Core constructs

### Cohesion

The degree to which group members feel bonded, loyal, mutually dependent, and motivated to maintain harmony.

### Leadership directiveness

The degree to which a leader signals a preferred outcome, controls discussion, discourages disagreement, or frames dissent as disloyal.

### Group insulation

The degree to which the group is isolated from outside evidence, dissenting expertise, competing perspectives, affected stakeholders, or external review.

### Stress level

The perceived pressure, crisis intensity, time constraint, threat, uncertainty, or decision urgency surrounding the group.

### Consensus pressure

The social pressure to align with an emerging group position.

### Self-censorship

Withholding doubts, objections, warnings, or alternative interpretations because dissent feels socially costly.

### Illusion of unanimity

Perception that agreement is stronger than it actually is, often because silence is misread as consent.

### Mindguarding

Filtering, withholding, minimizing, or discrediting information that could disrupt the group’s consensus.

### Alternative search breadth

The extent to which the group identifies and seriously evaluates multiple viable courses of action.

### Risk analysis quality

The extent to which risks, uncertainties, assumptions, downstream harms, and failure modes are evaluated.

### Contingency planning

Whether the group prepares fallback plans, monitoring triggers, and corrective procedures.

### Institutional safeguards

Procedures that make dissent legitimate and correction possible, such as devil’s advocacy, independent expert review, subgroup analysis, red teams, pre-mortems, minority reports, and post-decision review.

## Recommended designs

1. Directive versus impartial leadership manipulation.
2. High-cohesion versus moderate-cohesion group design.
3. Group-insulation versus outside-expert condition.
4. Stress/time-pressure manipulation.
5. Dissent-visible versus dissent-suppressed condition.
6. Devil’s advocate intervention.
7. Independent expert consultation intervention.
8. Subgroup review / second-chance meeting design.
9. Crisis decision simulation.
10. Longitudinal decision-quality audit.

## Measurement cautions

- Do not treat cohesion alone as groupthink.
- Measure process distortions, not only bad outcomes.
- Distinguish consensus from true agreement.
- Measure private reservations separately from public statements.
- Measure leadership behavior separately from group cohesion.
- Measure informational diversity and outside review.
- Measure whether alternatives were actually evaluated.
- Distinguish groupthink from group polarization.
- Include outcome quality, forecast calibration, risk realization, and post-decision learning.
- Account for hierarchy and unequal costs of dissent.

## Ethics

Groupthink research can involve hierarchical pressure, role-based stress, moral conflict, crisis simulations, and manipulation of dissent. Researchers should protect participants from reputational harm, debrief carefully, and avoid creating environments where participants feel genuinely coerced or punished.
