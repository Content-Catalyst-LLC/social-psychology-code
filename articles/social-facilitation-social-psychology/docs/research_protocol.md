# Research Protocol: Social Facilitation

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on social facilitation: the effect of social presence, audience observation, coaction, evaluation apprehension, and distraction on task performance.

It supports laboratory experiments, classroom studies, workplace-observation studies, digital monitoring designs, performance-under-evaluation experiments, human-computer interaction studies, and sport/performance psychology research.

## Core constructs

### Audience presence

Whether others are physically or virtually present while the participant performs a task.

### Coaction

Whether others perform the same or a similar task at the same time.

### Evaluation pressure

Perceived likelihood that performance is being judged, rated, compared, or recorded.

### Task difficulty

Cognitive, motor, or procedural complexity of the task.

### Task mastery

Degree to which the task is practiced, automatic, familiar, or well learned.

### Dominant-response correctness

Whether the participant's most likely response is correct for the task.

### Arousal index

Physiological or subjective activation during task performance.

### Distraction index

Attentional pull away from task demands because of observers, coactors, comparison, or social concern.

### Attentional conflict

Conflict between attending to the task and attending to the social environment.

### Perceived scrutiny

Subjective experience of being watched, assessed, monitored, or compared.

### Performance score

Task outcome: accuracy, productivity, speed, error-corrected score, completion rate, or expert rating.

### Response time

Latency or task completion time after filtering implausibly short responses.

### Digital monitoring

Computerized observation, dashboard visibility, screen monitoring, camera presence, performance analytics, or remote co-presence.

## Recommended designs

1. Alone versus audience-present experiment.
2. Alone versus coaction experiment.
3. Audience present with low versus high evaluation pressure.
4. Audience expertise manipulation.
5. Supportive versus critical audience manipulation.
6. Simple versus complex task manipulation.
7. Practiced versus unfamiliar task manipulation.
8. Physiological arousal and response-time measurement.
9. Digital monitoring versus private performance design.
10. Mixed design with repeated tasks nested within participants.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish social facilitation from social loafing.
- Distinguish mere presence from evaluation apprehension.
- Distinguish audience effects from coaction effects.
- Measure task mastery rather than assuming task simplicity.
- Measure perceived scrutiny, not only objective observer presence.
- Include baseline skill and prior practice.
- Model response-time distributions carefully.
- Avoid interpreting performance improvement as universally positive; arousal can increase error on complex tasks.
- For digital work settings, distinguish monitoring, accountability, surveillance, feedback, and social support.

## Ethics

Observation and evaluation studies can heighten anxiety or stress. Digital monitoring designs should avoid deceptive or coercive surveillance. Researchers should disclose monitoring conditions when possible, debrief participants, and avoid drawing character judgments from performance under observation.
