# Research Protocol: Altruism

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on altruism: behavior intended to benefit another individual, group, or public good at a potential cost to the actor.

It supports empathy-altruism experiments, costly-helping paradigms, donation studies, volunteerism research, public-goods games, kin-selection and reciprocity modeling, altruistic punishment experiments, moral-identity research, and institutional studies of prosocial contribution.

## Core constructs

### Altruistic decision

Whether the actor chooses a costly behavior intended to benefit another person, group, or public good.

### Empathic concern

Other-oriented emotional concern for the welfare of a person in need.

### Personal distress

Self-oriented aversive arousal caused by witnessing another's suffering.

### Helping cost

Material, time, social, reputational, physical, opportunity, or psychological cost of helping.

### Recipient need

Severity or urgency of the recipient's welfare deficit.

### Recipient closeness

Social, relational, kin, communal, or psychological closeness to the recipient.

### Identity overlap

Perceived overlap between self and recipient, group, or moral community.

### Kinship coefficient

Estimated genetic relatedness or kin-category proxy.

### Reciprocity expectation

Expectation that help may be returned directly or indirectly.

### Reputation visibility

Degree to which helping can be seen by others and affect status, trust, or moral image.

### Warm-glow expectation

Expected positive affect or self-signaling benefit from helping.

### Moral identity

Degree to which helping and care are central to one's self-concept.

### Social norm salience

Visibility and strength of norms that helping is expected or valued.

### Perceived efficacy

Belief that one's help will actually improve the recipient's welfare or public good.

### Altruistic punishment

Costly punishment of defectors, free riders, or norm violators in order to protect cooperation.

## Recommended designs

1. Empathy induction crossed with helping cost.
2. Easy-escape versus difficult-escape helping paradigm.
3. Anonymous donation versus publicly visible donation.
4. Kinship/closeness manipulation.
5. Reciprocity expectation manipulation.
6. Moral identity priming.
7. Public-goods contribution game.
8. Altruistic punishment experiment.
9. Effective-altruism style efficacy manipulation.
10. Institutional norm salience and trust manipulation.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish behavior from motivation.
- Distinguish empathic concern from personal distress.
- Measure expected personal reward rather than assuming its absence.
- Include helping cost and opportunity cost.
- Measure perceived efficacy of helping.
- Include recipient need and recipient identity.
- Separate genuine other-regarding concern from reputation visibility and warm glow.
- Recognize mixed motives as common rather than treating them as measurement failure.
- Avoid treating unpaid or sacrificial labor as morally uncomplicated altruism when institutional power is involved.

## Ethics

Altruism research can involve distressing scenarios, deception, donation decisions, economic games, and vulnerable recipients. Researchers should protect participants, avoid coercive donation pressure, ensure debriefing, and avoid using altruism language to exploit unpaid or marginalized labor.
