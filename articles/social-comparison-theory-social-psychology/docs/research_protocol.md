# Research Protocol: Social Comparison Theory

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on social comparison as a process of self-evaluation, motivation, affective response, norm inference, identity formation, and institutional benchmarking. It is designed for survey experiments, diary studies, organizational studies, digital media research, motivation studies, self-esteem research, and longitudinal comparison dynamics.

## Core constructs

### Comparison type

Whether the comparison is upward, downward, lateral, temporal, group-based, digital, organizational, or institutional.

### Comparison direction

Whether the reference target is perceived as better off, worse off, similar, or ambiguous on the focal dimension.

### Comparison domain

The domain of evaluation, such as ability, income, appearance, status, morality, productivity, health, achievement, or belonging.

### Reference group

The social group or category used as a benchmark for self-evaluation.

### Attainability

Degree to which the comparison target's standing seems reachable.

### Similarity

Degree to which the comparison target is perceived as relevant or similar to the participant.

### Identity relevance

Degree to which the domain matters to the participant's self-concept.

### Perceived gap

Difference between perceived self-standing and comparison target standing.

### Self-evaluation change

Change in self-evaluation from pre-comparison to post-comparison.

### Motivation

Effort, intention, aspiration, persistence, or goal engagement following comparison.

### Affective response

Emotional response such as envy, inspiration, discouragement, reassurance, shame, pride, gratitude, or resentment.

### Relative deprivation

Perceived disadvantage relative to a relevant standard accompanied by grievance, anger, or resentment.

### Norm perception

Inference about what is normal, expected, or valued in a group.

## Recommended designs

1. Upward/downward comparison experiment.
2. Attainability manipulation study.
3. Identity relevance by comparison direction design.
4. Digital feed exposure experiment.
5. Experience-sampling or diary study of everyday comparison.
6. Organizational salary/status comparison study.
7. Relative deprivation survey or experiment.
8. Reference-group manipulation study.
9. Longitudinal self-evaluation and motivation study.
10. Institutional benchmarking or ranking study.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish upward comparison from threatening comparison.
- Distinguish downward comparison from contempt or distancing.
- Measure attainability separately from comparison direction.
- Measure similarity and identity relevance.
- Separate affective responses from behavioral motivation.
- Distinguish passive digital exposure from active comparison seeking.
- Measure social comparison orientation as an individual difference where relevant.
- Avoid treating all comparison as harmful.
- Model repeated exposure and cumulative effects.
- Consider institutional systems that create comparison pressure.

## Ethics

Social comparison research can involve sensitive domains such as body image, income, professional achievement, health, status, race, gender, disability, class, or social exclusion. Researchers should avoid inducing unnecessary shame, humiliation, or distress and should debrief participants when comparison manipulations may affect self-evaluation.
