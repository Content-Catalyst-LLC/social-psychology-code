# Research Protocol: Collective Action and Social Change

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on collective action as coordinated participation aimed at changing social, institutional, political, organizational, or cultural conditions. It is designed for survey experiments, longitudinal panel studies, social movement research, social identity research, political psychology, protest event analysis, online mobilization studies, network diffusion studies, and mixed-methods research.

## Core constructs

### Social identity strength

Degree to which the participant identifies with a group, movement, constituency, community, or cause-relevant collective.

### Perceived injustice

Degree to which the participant sees the status quo, event, policy, treatment, or institutional arrangement as unfair, illegitimate, discriminatory, exploitative, or morally unacceptable.

### Moral outrage

Group-based anger, indignation, resentment, or moral urgency directed toward perceived injustice.

### Collective efficacy

Belief that coordinated group action can meaningfully affect institutions, policy, public opinion, organizational behavior, or social norms.

### Network support

Interpersonal, organizational, or digital ties that provide information, validation, resources, encouragement, recruitment, and coordination.

### Mobilization exposure

Exposure to calls for action, organizing messages, protest frames, digital campaigns, meetings, organizing conversations, or movement events.

### Participation cost

Expected personal cost of participation, including time, money, reputational risk, physical risk, employment risk, legal risk, social conflict, or emotional burden.

### Perceived repression risk

Expected risk of policing, retaliation, surveillance, sanctions, institutional discipline, online harassment, or other punitive response.

### Free-rider incentive

Degree to which a participant can benefit from collective outcomes without personally participating.

### Participation intention

Self-reported intention to participate in collective action.

### Actual participation

Observed or self-reported participation in an action, protest, strike, petition, meeting, mutual-aid effort, campaign, digital action, or organizing task.

### Digital engagement

Shares, posts, messages, petition signatures, donation links, event RSVPs, hashtag usage, or other digital mobilization behaviors.

### Offline engagement

Attendance, canvassing, protest participation, organizing meetings, mutual aid, strike participation, workplace organizing, or public testimony.

### Institutional response

Policy change, concession, repression, symbolic response, negotiation, co-optation, escalation, or nonresponse.

## Recommended designs

1. Cross-sectional survey of collective action intention.
2. Panel study tracking identity, efficacy, outrage, and participation over time.
3. Survey experiment manipulating injustice, efficacy, or identity salience.
4. Network diffusion design measuring recruitment through ties.
5. Protest-event dataset linked to institutional response.
6. Digital mobilization study measuring exposure and engagement.
7. Mixed-methods study combining interviews, survey data, and event logs.
8. Comparative study across movements, organizations, or localities.
9. Repression-risk study examining participation under threat.
10. Human-AI or platform study examining algorithmic visibility and mobilization.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish identity strength from ideological agreement.
- Distinguish perceived injustice from personal dissatisfaction.
- Measure efficacy at the collective level, not only individual confidence.
- Separate intention from actual participation.
- Record participation cost and repression risk.
- Model network dependence and clustering.
- Avoid treating digital engagement as equivalent to durable organizing.
- Distinguish symbolic support from costly participation.
- Treat collective action as potentially emancipatory, reactionary, exclusionary, or authoritarian depending on goals and context.
- Combine social-psychological predictors with institutional opportunity, repression, resources, and political context.

## Ethical considerations

Collective action research can involve politically vulnerable populations. Researchers should protect confidentiality, avoid exposing participants to retaliation, handle protest and network data carefully, minimize surveillance risk, and avoid publishing identifiable movement information without consent. Studies involving marginalized groups, repression, workplace organizing, immigration status, policing, or authoritarian contexts require heightened care.
