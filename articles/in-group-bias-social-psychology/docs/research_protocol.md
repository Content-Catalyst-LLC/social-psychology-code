# Research Protocol: In-Group Bias

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on in-group bias as preferential evaluation, trust, empathy, resource allocation, moral judgment, and institutional treatment of members of one's own group relative to outgroup members. It is designed for minimal-group experiments, trust games, allocation tasks, vignette experiments, organizational decision studies, political psychology, intergroup conflict research, and institutional equity audits.

## Core constructs

### In-group target

Whether the target person belongs to the participant's group or identity category.

### Identity salience

Degree to which group identity is made psychologically active in the task.

### Group identification

Degree to which the participant identifies with the relevant group.

### Perceived threat

Degree to which the outgroup or situation is perceived as threatening the status, safety, norms, values, or resources of the ingroup.

### Norm strength

Degree to which loyalty, solidarity, reciprocity, or preferential support for the ingroup is perceived as expected.

### Status asymmetry

Degree to which group relations are unequal in power, legitimacy, resources, or institutional standing.

### Trust rating

Participant's trust in the target.

### Fairness rating

Participant's perception that the target deserves fair treatment or has acted fairly.

### Moral blame

Degree of blame assigned to a target after a negative act.

### Moral forgiveness

Willingness to excuse, contextualize, or forgive a target's negative act.

### Punishment severity

Severity of sanction assigned to the target.

### Resource allocation

Material or symbolic resources allocated to the target.

### Cooperation choice

Whether the participant chooses to cooperate with the target.

## Recommended designs

1. Minimal-group allocation experiment.
2. Trust-game experiment with in-group and outgroup targets.
3. Moral-vignette experiment with identical behavior by in-group and outgroup actors.
4. Hiring, promotion, grading, or discipline audit with randomized group cues.
5. Political partisanship judgment experiment.
6. Resource allocation task under threat and non-threat conditions.
7. Longitudinal group identity and polarization study.
8. Cross-cultural or cross-institutional comparison of favoritism norms.
9. Network study of homophily, trust, and preferential opportunity.
10. Mixed-methods organizational study combining decisions, interviews, and administrative data.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish in-group favoritism from outgroup derogation.
- Measure positive treatment, not only negative attitudes.
- Separate explicit bias from implicit or behavioral preference.
- Record whether the task involves resource scarcity.
- Measure identity salience and perceived threat separately.
- Consider whether group solidarity is protective, exclusionary, or both.
- Evaluate institutional consequences, not only individual attitudes.
- Avoid treating marginalized group solidarity as morally equivalent to dominant-group exclusion.
- Examine asymmetry by power and status.
- Consider repeated decision systems where small favoritism effects accumulate.

## Ethics

In-group bias studies can expose sensitive identity dynamics. Researchers should avoid reinforcing stereotypes, protect participants from harm, be careful with politically or ethnically charged categories, and interpret findings within structural context rather than treating all group attachments as equivalent.
