# Research Protocol: Prosocial Behavior

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on prosocial behavior: actions intended to benefit others or collective welfare, including helping, sharing, cooperation, volunteering, donation, comforting, civic participation, mutual aid, and public-goods contribution.

It supports laboratory experiments, vignette studies, field studies, organizational citizenship research, public-health cooperation research, donation studies, online-platform studies, mutual-aid research, and public-goods experiments.

## Core constructs

### Prosocial behavior

A broad class of actions intended to benefit other individuals, groups, communities, or social systems.

### Altruism

A subset of prosocial behavior in which costly helping is substantially motivated by concern for another's welfare.

### Empathic concern

Other-oriented emotional concern for a person or group in need.

### Perspective taking

Cognitive capacity to imagine or understand another person's situation.

### Social norm salience

Visibility and perceived strength of norms that helping, sharing, cooperation, or responsibility are expected.

### Reciprocity expectation

Expectation that prosocial action may later be returned directly or indirectly.

### Perceived efficacy

Belief that one's action will actually improve another's welfare or contribute to a public good.

### Helping cost

Material, time, effort, opportunity, social, reputational, or risk-related cost of helping.

### Bystander count

Actual or perceived number of other people who could also help.

### Felt responsibility

Personal sense of responsibility to act.

### Identity overlap

Perceived closeness or shared identity with the person or group in need.

### Institutional legitimacy

Degree to which institutions asking for cooperation are trusted, fair, accountable, and worthy of support.

### Cooperation contribution

Contribution to a group task, public good, shared pool, community effort, or institutional objective.

## Recommended designs

1. Empathy induction crossed with helping cost.
2. Social norm salience manipulation.
3. Reciprocity expectation manipulation.
4. Perceived efficacy manipulation.
5. Alone versus multiple-bystander helping design.
6. In-group versus out-group recipient design.
7. Anonymous versus public helping design.
8. Public-goods contribution game.
9. Organizational citizenship / voluntary helping survey.
10. Institutional legitimacy and public-health cooperation experiment.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish prosocial behavior from altruism.
- Measure behavior, not only intention.
- Include cost and perceived efficacy.
- Measure bystander count and felt responsibility.
- Distinguish empathic concern from personal distress.
- Include recipient identity and group boundaries.
- Include institutional trust and legitimacy when cooperation is requested by public authorities.
- Avoid interpreting all helping as pure virtue or all self-benefit as cynicism.
- Recognize that prosocial expectations can be exploitative when burdens fall unequally.

## Ethics

Prosocial-behavior research can involve distressing scenarios, donation decisions, group pressure, public-health messaging, online harm, and vulnerable recipients. Researchers should avoid coercion, protect confidentiality, debrief participants, and avoid using prosocial language to pressure unpaid or marginalized labor.
