# Research Protocol: Contact Hypothesis and Intergroup Contact

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on intergroup contact as a mechanism for prejudice reduction, stereotype revision, anxiety reduction, empathy growth, trust formation, and inclusive norm development. It is designed for survey experiments, longitudinal school/workplace/community studies, intergroup dialogue research, conflict transformation programs, post-conflict contact studies, and digital or indirect contact research.

## Core constructs

### Contact frequency

How often participants interact with members of the target outgroup.

### Contact quality

Whether contact is positive, meaningful, cooperative, respectful, voluntary, and sustained.

### Equal status

Whether groups occupy equal standing within the contact setting.

### Common goals

Whether participants share a goal that requires or encourages joint effort.

### Cooperation

Whether contact is collaborative rather than competitive.

### Institutional support

Whether authorities, policies, norms, educators, managers, facilitators, or laws support respectful interaction.

### Negative contact

Hostile, humiliating, threatening, coercive, competitive, or degrading contact that may increase prejudice.

### Indirect contact

Contact that does not involve direct interaction, such as extended contact, vicarious contact, parasocial contact, or imagined contact.

### Intergroup anxiety

Anticipated discomfort, fear, uncertainty, or threat during intergroup interaction.

### Empathy and perspective taking

Affective and cognitive capacity to understand the experience of outgroup members.

### Trust

Expectation that outgroup members are safe, reliable, cooperative, or legitimate partners in interaction.

### Prejudice score

Explicit or implicit negative evaluation of an outgroup.

### Social distance

Willingness to live near, work with, befriend, cooperate with, or accept outgroup members in valued social roles.

## Recommended designs

1. Randomized intergroup-contact intervention with pre/post measurement.
2. Longitudinal school or workplace contact study.
3. Survey experiment manipulating Allport conditions.
4. Intergroup dialogue program with repeated waves.
5. Cross-sectional contact-quality survey with mediation modeling.
6. Negative-contact study distinguishing positive and negative valence.
7. Indirect-contact design measuring extended, imagined, or vicarious contact.
8. Network study of cross-group ties and norm diffusion.
9. Multi-site institutional study comparing support, equality, and cooperation conditions.
10. Mixed-methods study combining interviews, surveys, and program records.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish contact frequency from contact quality.
- Measure negative contact separately from positive contact.
- Treat equal status as setting-specific, not only societal status.
- Record whether contact is voluntary or coerced.
- Distinguish individual prejudice reduction from structural equality.
- Measure institutional support explicitly.
- Avoid placing the burden of prejudice reduction only on marginalized participants.
- Consider asymmetric effects for dominant and marginalized groups.
- Use longitudinal designs when possible.
- Distinguish immediate attitude change from durable norm change.

## Ethics

Intergroup-contact research can involve vulnerable participants, minoritized groups, trauma histories, hostile environments, or unequal power. Researchers should protect confidentiality, avoid forced disclosure, monitor harm, avoid tokenizing marginalized participants, and ensure that contact does not reproduce unequal status or expose participants to hostility.
