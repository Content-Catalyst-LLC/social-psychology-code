# Research Protocol: Social Loafing

## Purpose

This protocol supports research on social loafing: the reduction of individual effort in collective tasks relative to comparable solo or individually identifiable tasks. It is designed for laboratory group-effort studies, classroom group projects, organizational team research, public-goods analogues, and distributed digital collaboration.

## Core constructs

- **Group size:** number of contributors whose efforts are pooled.
- **Identifiability:** degree to which individual contributions can be attributed.
- **Accountability:** perceived likelihood of evaluation, feedback, or responsibility.
- **Task value:** importance or meaning of the collective outcome.
- **Task uniqueness:** perceived nonredundancy of one’s contribution.
- **Instrumentality:** belief that one’s effort meaningfully affects the group outcome.
- **Free-rider expectation:** belief that reduced effort can be hidden while still benefiting.
- **Sucker-effect concern:** fear of being exploited by lower contributors.
- **Social compensation:** increased effort to compensate for expected weak performance by others.
- **Coordination loss:** process loss caused by synchronization and communication difficulty.
- **Motivation loss:** effort reduction caused by weak accountability, low instrumentality, or diffuse responsibility.
- **Digital traceability:** visibility created by commits, version histories, task boards, shared docs, dashboards, or audit trails.

## Recommended designs

1. Solo versus pooled group performance.
2. Pooled group versus identifiable group contribution.
3. Small versus large group-size manipulation.
4. High versus low accountability.
5. High versus low task value.
6. Unique versus redundant contribution.
7. Co-located versus distributed team.
8. Low versus high digital traceability.
9. Free-rider expectation manipulation.
10. Sucker-effect and social-compensation design.

## Measurement cautions

- Separate coordination loss from motivation loss.
- Include solo baseline effort where possible.
- Do not interpret all low effort as laziness; examine task design, role clarity, workload, power, incentives, and group norms.
- Measure identifiability, accountability, task value, and perceived instrumentality directly.
- In digital teams, measure contribution traceability separately from surveillance.
- Include social compensation as a counter-process; some members work harder when group performance is threatened.
