# Research Protocol: Moral Disengagement

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on moral disengagement as a process through which moral self-sanctions are selectively weakened, bypassed, or cognitively restructured. It is designed for harmful-choice experiments, organizational ethics studies, political violence research, environmental harm studies, institutional corruption research, dehumanization research, and public-policy judgment studies.

## Core constructs

### Moral justification

Degree to which harmful conduct is framed as serving a higher good, noble purpose, protective function, necessary reform, security goal, efficiency imperative, or moral duty.

### Euphemistic labeling

Degree to which harmful conduct is softened through sanitizing language, bureaucratic wording, technical labels, or abstractions that reduce perceived severity.

### Advantageous comparison

Degree to which harmful conduct is made to appear acceptable by comparison with something worse.

### Displacement of responsibility

Degree to which responsibility is shifted to authority figures, rules, orders, policies, procedures, markets, algorithms, institutions, or leadership.

### Diffusion of responsibility

Degree to which responsibility is spread across groups, committees, systems, departments, supply chains, bureaucracies, or collective processes.

### Distortion of consequences

Degree to which harm is minimized, denied, hidden, abstracted, delayed, statistically diluted, or made less visible.

### Dehumanization

Degree to which targets of harm are described as less fully human, dangerous, inferior, contaminating, undeserving, faceless, expendable, or outside moral community.

### Attribution of blame

Degree to which victims or targets are blamed for the harm they experience.

### Harm visibility

Degree to which the consequences of action are vivid, proximate, concrete, and identifiable.

### Perceived agency

Degree to which the actor sees themselves as personally able to choose otherwise.

### Responsibility clarity

Degree to which responsibility for harm is traceable to specific actors, decisions, policies, or institutional structures.

### Harmful decision

Behavioral or stated willingness to support, enact, endorse, excuse, or ignore harmful conduct.

## Recommended designs

1. Harmful-choice experiment with mechanism manipulation.
2. Organizational misconduct vignette study.
3. Authority-pressure and responsibility-displacement experiment.
4. Diffusion-of-responsibility group decision task.
5. Consequence-visibility manipulation.
6. Dehumanization and empathy-reduction experiment.
7. Environmental externality or supply-chain harm study.
8. Political violence or security-policy endorsement experiment.
9. Whistleblowing and dissent-protection study.
10. Longitudinal institutional normalization simulation.

## Measurement cautions

- Measure mechanisms separately rather than using only a global index.
- Distinguish dispositional moral-disengagement propensity from state moral-disengagement process.
- Separate moral disengagement from sincere moral disagreement.
- Measure harm visibility and perceived agency.
- Measure institutional pressure, authority pressure, and group norms.
- Include empathy, guilt, responsibility clarity, and learning/accountability outcomes.
- Avoid treating all moral disagreement as disengagement.
- Avoid using moral disengagement as a total explanation of structural harm.
- Consider power, coercion, incentives, ideology, and institutional dependency.

## Ethics

Moral disengagement research can involve harmful scenarios, dehumanizing language, political violence, corporate misconduct, environmental harm, or victim blame. Researchers should avoid normalizing harmful language, minimize participant distress, debrief participants, and interpret findings in relation to institutions and power rather than treating harm as only individual pathology.
