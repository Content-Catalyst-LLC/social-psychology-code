# Research Protocol: Prisoner's Dilemma

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on prisoner’s dilemma games as models of strategic interdependence, cooperation under risk, reciprocity, trust, retaliation, institutional enforcement, and collective-action failure. It is designed for one-shot and repeated games, dyadic experiments, online behavioral studies, organizational cooperation tasks, intergroup simulations, and institutional-design research.

## Core constructs

### Cooperation

Choice that supports mutual welfare but creates vulnerability to exploitation.

### Defection

Choice that protects or benefits the individual at the partner’s expense, especially when the partner cooperates.

### Payoff structure

Temptation \(T\), reward \(R\), punishment \(P\), and sucker’s payoff \(S\), satisfying \(T > R > P > S\), with mutual cooperation often requiring \(2R > T + S\).

### Trust

Belief that the partner is likely to cooperate.

### Fairness

Judgment that outcomes, procedures, or partner behavior are fair.

### Expected partner cooperation

Subjective probability that the partner will cooperate.

### Reciprocity

Tendency to cooperate after cooperation and defect after defection.

### Forgiveness

Tendency to return to cooperation after partner defection, especially when errors or noise are possible.

### Reputation visibility

Degree to which behavior is observable across future partners or rounds.

### Monitoring strength

Ability to detect defection reliably.

### Institutional enforcement

Sanctions, contracts, rules, third-party oversight, or governance mechanisms that change the expected payoff of defection.

### Communication access

Whether players can communicate, signal intentions, make promises, or coordinate expectations.

### Social identity salience

Whether the partner is perceived as an in-group member, out-group member, stranger, rival, teammate, or institutional counterpart.

## Recommended designs

1. One-shot prisoner’s dilemma experiment.
2. Repeated prisoner’s dilemma with fixed or uncertain horizon.
3. Trust and expectation manipulation.
4. Communication/no-communication condition.
5. Reputation visibility manipulation.
6. Punishment and sanction availability design.
7. Monitoring and verification experiment.
8. Social identity and intergroup cooperation design.
9. Noisy repeated-game simulation.
10. Institutional enforcement and collective-action design.

## Measurement cautions

- Confirm that the payoff matrix satisfies prisoner’s dilemma inequalities.
- Distinguish cooperation from altruism; cooperation may be strategic.
- Distinguish one-shot from repeated-game incentives.
- Measure expectations and trust, not only choices.
- Include partner history and lagged behavior in repeated designs.
- Treat communication, identity, and institutions as part of the perceived game.
- Avoid reducing all cooperation problems to prisoner’s dilemmas.
- Compare with assurance games, coordination games, public-goods games, and chicken/hawk-dove where appropriate.

## Ethics

Prisoner’s dilemma research often involves deception, competition, punishment, scarcity, betrayal, and financial incentives. Researchers should debrief participants, avoid excessive inducements, clarify payment rules, and treat cooperation/defection as behavior under incentives rather than moral character.
