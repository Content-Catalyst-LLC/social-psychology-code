# Research Protocol: Self-Serving Bias

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on the self-serving bias as an attributional asymmetry in explanations of success and failure. It is designed for attribution experiments, performance feedback studies, organizational learning research, leadership accountability studies, team conflict studies, political judgment experiments, and institutional responsibility analysis.

## Core constructs

### Outcome valence

Whether the focal outcome is positive, negative, mixed, or ambiguous.

### Actor target

Whether the outcome concerns the self, another individual, a team, a leader, an institution, or an outgroup.

### Internal attribution

Degree to which the cause is attributed to ability, effort, character, skill, leadership, or competence.

### External attribution

Degree to which the cause is attributed to luck, task difficulty, unfair constraints, market conditions, institutional barriers, or situational pressure.

### Stable attribution

Degree to which the cause is viewed as enduring rather than temporary.

### Controllable attribution

Degree to which the cause is viewed as under the actor's control.

### Responsibility rating

Degree to which responsibility is assigned to the actor.

### Blame rating

Degree to which blame is assigned after a negative outcome.

### Credit claiming

Degree to which the actor claims personal credit for a positive outcome.

### Excuse making

Degree to which the actor deflects responsibility for a negative outcome.

### Ego threat

Degree to which the outcome threatens identity, competence, morality, or status.

### Task importance

Degree to which the outcome matters to self-concept or public standing.

### Accountability pressure

Degree to which the actor expects to justify the attribution to others or to evidence.

### Learning intention

Willingness to revise behavior, seek feedback, or change strategy after an outcome.

## Recommended designs

1. Success/failure feedback experiment.
2. Self-versus-other attribution design.
3. Ambiguous responsibility vignette study.
4. Leadership accountability experiment.
5. Team project attribution conflict study.
6. Political outcome attribution experiment.
7. Organizational postmortem or incident-review study.
8. Accountability-pressure manipulation.
9. Culture and self-construal comparison study.
10. Longitudinal learning-from-failure study.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish self-enhancement after success from self-protection after failure.
- Measure internal and external attribution separately.
- Measure responsibility and blame separately.
- Include objective evidence strength where possible.
- Distinguish legitimate situational constraints from defensive excuse making.
- Avoid treating all external attribution as biased.
- Include accountability and evidence-review conditions.
- Consider cultural and institutional norms.
- Distinguish individual self-serving bias from organizational credit/blame narratives.
- Model repeated decision consequences for learning and accountability.

## Ethics

Self-serving bias research can involve failure feedback, ego threat, responsibility, blame, and moral self-evaluation. Researchers should avoid unnecessary humiliation, provide debriefing, distinguish experimental manipulation from actual ability assessment, and take care when studying groups or cultures not to imply moral deficiency.
