# Research Protocol: Obedience, Authority, and Social Power

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on obedience, authority, hierarchy, legitimacy, compliance, responsibility displacement, moral conflict, peer dissent, resistance, and institutional safeguards.

It is intended for ethically modified obedience studies, survey experiments, vignette studies, virtual simulations, organizational case coding, archival research, role-based decision experiments, and computational modeling of compliance under authority.

## Core constructs

### Authority legitimacy

The perceived rightfulness of the authority figure, office, institution, profession, protocol, or command structure.

### Authority proximity

The physical, social, or communicative immediacy of the authority figure issuing a directive.

### Institutional prestige

The perceived credibility, expertise, status, or legitimacy of the institutional setting.

### Command clarity

The extent to which the directive is explicit, unambiguous, and framed as expected behavior.

### Cost of defiance

The perceived social, professional, legal, reputational, or emotional cost of refusing the directive.

### Escalation step

The incremental stage of compliance, especially where small acts create commitment to later acts.

### Responsibility displacement

The degree to which the participant perceives responsibility as belonging to the authority, institution, protocol, or system rather than the self.

### Moral conflict

The degree of internal discomfort, ethical concern, distress, hesitation, or perceived wrongness.

### Harm salience

The visibility, concreteness, and emotional immediacy of harm or potential harm.

### Peer dissent

Visible refusal, hesitation, objection, or moral questioning by others.

### Role identification

Identification with the assigned role, such as teacher, technician, officer, clinician, analyst, employee, or researcher.

### Mission identification

Identification with the stated goal of the authority or institution, such as science, safety, security, order, efficiency, service, or public good.

### Resistance

Refusal, delay, questioning, protest, appeal to another authority, disclosure, whistleblowing, or protective action.

## Recommended designs

1. High-legitimacy versus low-legitimacy authority manipulation.
2. Authority-present versus authority-distant condition.
3. Peer-dissent versus peer-compliance condition.
4. High-responsibility versus responsibility-displacement condition.
5. Visible-harm versus abstract-harm condition.
6. Escalating-request sequence.
7. Mission-identification manipulation.
8. Role-identification manipulation.
9. Institutional-prestige manipulation.
10. Resistance-support intervention.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish obedience from private agreement.
- Measure hesitation, protest, negotiation, and partial resistance, not only final compliance.
- Distinguish responsibility displacement from simple conformity.
- Measure identity with the mission separately from authority pressure.
- Measure visible harm and victim proximity.
- Measure peer dissent as a social affordance for resistance.
- Treat direct Milgram-style replication as ethically restricted; use modified, lower-risk paradigms.
- Do not treat explanation as exoneration. Situational analysis should deepen institutional accountability.

## Ethics

Obedience research may involve authority pressure, deception, moral discomfort, and perceived harm. Researchers should use ethically modified designs, informed consent where possible, careful debriefing, participant withdrawal protections, and institutional review. The goal is to study authority without unnecessarily reproducing coercive authority.
