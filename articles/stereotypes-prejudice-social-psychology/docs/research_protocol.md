# Research Protocol: Stereotypes, Prejudice, and Discrimination

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on stereotypes, prejudice, discrimination, social categorization, stereotype content, social identity, implicit-explicit bias, intergroup conflict, contact, stereotype threat, and institutional disparity.

It supports vignette experiments, audit-style decision tasks, stereotype-content surveys, intergroup-contact studies, stereotype-threat experiments, organizational evaluations, education studies, healthcare or public-policy decision studies, and simulations of cumulative inequality.

## Core constructs

### Stereotype

A cognitive belief, expectation, or association about members of a social group.

### Prejudice

An evaluative attitude or affective orientation toward a group or its members.

### Discrimination

A behavior, decision, practice, or institutional process that disadvantages a group.

### Warmth and competence

Two core dimensions in stereotype-content research. Warmth concerns perceived intent; competence concerns perceived capacity.

### Perceived threat

The perception that an outgroup threatens resources, values, security, status, identity, or cultural dominance.

### Perceived competition

The perception that groups compete over material resources, status, access, recognition, power, or institutional opportunity.

### Intergroup contact

Interaction between members of different groups, especially under conditions of equal status, cooperation, shared goals, and institutional support.

### Stereotype threat

The performance pressure created when a negative stereotype about one's group is salient in an evaluative context.

### Institutional disparity

Cumulative unequal outcomes produced by repeated decisions, rules, categories, or practices.

## Recommended designs

1. Stereotype-content rating design using warmth, competence, status, and competition.
2. Prejudice-rating design measuring affective evaluation and social distance.
3. Discrimination-tendency vignette design with randomized target-group cues.
4. Intergroup-contact design measuring contact quality, equal status, cooperation, and institutional support.
5. Stereotype-threat experiment with threat-salience manipulation.
6. Implicit-explicit comparison design.
7. Audit-style evaluation design in hiring, education, healthcare, discipline, or public administration.
8. Longitudinal design measuring cumulative decision disparity.
9. Intervention design testing structured criteria, contact, perspective-taking, and accountability.
10. Multi-level model separating individual, group, scenario, and institutional effects.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish stereotypes, prejudice, and discrimination.
- Distinguish cognitive belief, affective evaluation, and behavior.
- Do not infer prejudice from group difference alone without design support.
- Measure power and institutional discretion where possible.
- Avoid treating marginalized groups as passive objects of bias.
- Separate explicit attitudes from implicit associations.
- Distinguish contact quantity from contact quality.
- Measure institutional support for contact, not only interpersonal exposure.
- In stereotype-threat studies, measure threat salience, anxiety, working-memory load, and task framing.
- Report reliability, preregistered hypotheses, exclusion rules, and sensitivity analyses.

## Ethics

Research on stereotypes and prejudice involves sensitive social categories and histories of harm. Researchers should avoid reinforcing stereotypes through careless measurement, protect participant privacy, describe categories respectfully, debrief carefully, and ensure that findings are not used to stigmatize marginalized groups.
