# Research Protocol: Tragedy of the Commons

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on tragedy-of-the-commons dynamics: situations in which shared, rivalrous resources degrade when individual extraction incentives are stronger than collective restraint and governance is absent, weak, or illegitimate.

It supports commons-extraction experiments, common-pool resource studies, field governance assessments, open-access simulations, institutional-design experiments, polycentric governance models, and environmental social-psychology research.

## Core constructs

### Extraction

Amount removed from a shared resource by an actor in a given round or period.

### Sustainable threshold

Extraction level that can occur without depleting the resource below regenerative capacity.

### Resource stock

Current state of the shared resource.

### Regeneration

Resource recovery between periods.

### Property regime

Open access, common property, private property, state property, or hybrid/polycentric governance.

### Boundary clarity

Degree to which users and resource boundaries are defined.

### Rule participation

Degree to which users participate in rule design or revision.

### Monitoring credibility

Perceived likelihood that extraction behavior is observable.

### Sanction probability and severity

Likelihood and cost of sanction for excessive extraction.

### Institutional legitimacy

Perceived fairness, appropriateness, and acceptability of governance arrangements.

### Conflict-resolution access

Availability of low-cost mechanisms for resolving disputes.

### Local ecological knowledge

User knowledge of resource dynamics, limits, seasons, feedback, and sustainability conditions.

### Stewardship norm salience

Strength of cooperative norms around restraint, repair, restoration, and future responsibility.

### Reciprocity expectation

Belief that restraint will be met by restraint from others.

### Asymmetry

Inequality in power, need, access, responsibility, vulnerability, or historical benefit.

## Recommended designs

1. Open-access commons extraction game.
2. Governed commons experiment with rules and monitoring.
3. Communication/no-communication commons game.
4. Graduated sanctions and legitimacy manipulation.
5. Boundary clarity and user-rights manipulation.
6. Local rule-making versus externally imposed rule experiment.
7. Inequality/asymmetry commons design.
8. Intergenerational commons experiment.
9. Polycentric governance simulation.
10. Field survey of common-pool resource institutions.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish commons from open access.
- Measure governance quality rather than assuming all shared resources are unmanaged.
- Track resource stock and regeneration explicitly.
- Measure legitimacy separately from enforcement.
- Measure distributional fairness and asymmetry.
- Avoid treating overuse as only individual greed.
- Include local ecological knowledge and user participation.
- Include power and historical responsibility in large-scale environmental cases.
- Interpret commons failure in relation to institutions, not human nature alone.

## Ethics

Commons experiments may include scarcity, unequal payoff, sanctions, and resource-collapse scenarios. Researchers should debrief participants, avoid moralizing noncooperation, and distinguish behavior under incentives from character judgment.
