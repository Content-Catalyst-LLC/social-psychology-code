# Research Protocol: Group Polarization and Collective Judgment

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on group polarization and collective judgment: the tendency for discussion among like-minded people to move attitudes, confidence, and collective decisions toward more extreme positions in the direction of the group's initial inclination.

It supports laboratory discussion experiments, online-group studies, jury and legal deliberation research, organizational decision-making research, political communication studies, digital-platform research, institutional governance studies, and deliberative-design interventions.

## Core constructs

### Pre-discussion attitude

The individual attitude, judgment, risk preference, policy preference, or moral evaluation measured before discussion.

### Post-discussion attitude

The corresponding attitude or judgment measured after discussion.

### Attitude shift

Post-discussion attitude minus pre-discussion attitude.

### Directional polarization

A post-discussion shift farther in the direction of the group's initial mean.

### Extremity amplification

Increase in the absolute magnitude of attitudes, confidence, or decisions after discussion.

### Persuasive argument exposure

Quantity and strength of arguments encountered that support the dominant group position.

### Argument diversity

Degree to which discussion includes strong arguments on multiple sides.

### Informational homogeneity

Degree to which information flows within the group point in the same direction.

### Social comparison pressure

Pressure to adopt a position perceived as socially desirable, committed, sophisticated, courageous, morally serious, or loyal within the group.

### Identity salience

Degree to which group identity is active during discussion.

### Group identification

Strength of attachment to the group.

### Norm enforcement

Degree to which the group rewards alignment and punishes dissent.

### Dissent presence

Whether disagreement is present during discussion.

### Dissent quality

Whether dissent is informed, specific, protected, and engaged rather than merely tokenized.

### Deliberation structure

Institutional or procedural arrangement of discussion, including turn-taking, independent pre-commitment, devil's advocacy, structured evidence review, and minority-view protection.

### Algorithmic reinforcement

Degree to which recommendation systems, feeds, or digital ranking systems increase exposure to attitude-consistent content.

### Cross-cutting exposure

Exposure to credible arguments or participants from outside the group's dominant orientation.

### Collective judgment accuracy

Degree to which group decision quality improves or worsens after discussion.

## Recommended designs

1. Pre-post attitude design with group-discussion manipulation.
2. Homogeneous versus heterogeneous discussion groups.
3. Persuasive-argument exposure manipulation.
4. Social-comparison pressure manipulation.
5. Identity-salience manipulation.
6. Norm-enforcement manipulation.
7. Dissent-present versus dissent-absent condition.
8. Structured deliberation versus unstructured discussion.
9. Algorithmic reinforcement versus cross-cutting exposure.
10. Repeated-discussion longitudinal design.

## Measurement cautions

- Use directional measures, not only mean shift.
- Distinguish attitude extremity from confidence extremity.
- Measure the group mean before and after discussion.
- Include argument diversity and informational homogeneity.
- Distinguish informational influence from social comparison pressure.
- Measure identity salience and group identification separately.
- Measure dissent quality, not only dissent presence.
- Measure institutional safeguards and deliberation structure.
- Avoid assuming all polarization is irrational; some groups may move toward a more accurate position after pooling valid evidence.
- Distinguish polarization from groupthink: groupthink emphasizes suppression of critical scrutiny; polarization emphasizes directional amplification after discussion.

## Ethics

Polarization research can expose participants to political conflict, moralized identity, misinformation, extremist content, legal judgments, or hostile group discussion. Researchers should avoid intensifying harm, protect dissenting participants, debrief participants carefully, and avoid using interventions that amplify real-world extremist recruitment or harassment.
