# Research Protocol: Social Norms

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on social norms: perceived informal rules and shared expectations that guide behavior within groups, institutions, organizations, communities, and societies.

It supports norm-perception experiments, descriptive/injunctive norm-message studies, public-goods experiments, public-health cooperation studies, environmental behavior interventions, organizational culture research, social-change campaigns, development-program measurement, and institutional legitimacy research.

## Core constructs

### Descriptive norm

Perception of what people in a relevant reference group typically do.

### Injunctive norm

Perception of what people in a relevant reference group approve or disapprove.

### Empirical expectation

Belief about how many others comply with a behavior.

### Normative expectation

Belief about whether others expect one to comply.

### Conditional preference

Preference for compliance conditional on beliefs about others' behavior and expectations.

### Reference group

The group whose behavior, approval, expectations, or sanctions matter for the actor.

### Norm salience

Degree to which a norm is cognitively active in the moment of decision.

### Sanction salience

Visibility or anticipated likelihood of disapproval, exclusion, penalty, shame, or reputational cost.

### Reward salience

Visibility or anticipated likelihood of praise, status, belonging, trust, or reputational benefit.

### Pluralistic ignorance

A mismatch between private attitudes and perceived group norms.

### Dynamic norm

Perception that a behavior is increasing or decreasing, even if it is not currently dominant.

### Institutional legitimacy

Perceived fairness, competence, accountability, and trustworthiness of the institution associated with a norm.

### Norm tipping

A threshold process in which perceived changes in others' behavior or approval accelerate broader norm change.

## Recommended designs

1. Descriptive norm message versus control.
2. Injunctive norm message versus control.
3. Combined descriptive + injunctive message.
4. Dynamic norm message showing change over time.
5. Misperception correction experiment.
6. Public-goods contribution experiment.
7. Sanction visibility manipulation.
8. Reference-group identification manipulation.
9. Institutional legitimacy manipulation.
10. Threshold/tipping simulation or longitudinal panel design.

## Measurement cautions

- Specify the behavior precisely.
- Specify the reference group.
- Measure personal attitudes separately from perceived norms.
- Distinguish empirical expectations from normative expectations.
- Distinguish descriptive norms from injunctive norms.
- Measure sanction and reward expectations.
- Include perceived legitimacy and trust when norms are institutionally supported.
- Measure pluralistic ignorance when private attitudes and perceived norms may diverge.
- Avoid using norm messages that accidentally normalize harmful behavior.
- Avoid assuming that norms are always beneficial; many norms sustain exclusion or inequality.

## Ethics

Norm interventions can change behavior through social pressure. Researchers and practitioners should avoid manipulation, protect vulnerable groups, avoid public shaming, test for backfire effects, and ensure that norm-change efforts do not reinforce stigma, surveillance, or coercive compliance.
