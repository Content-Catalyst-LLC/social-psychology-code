# Research Protocol: Intergroup Conflict

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on intergroup conflict: tension, competition, distrust, hostility, avoidance, exclusion, aggression, and escalation between social groups.

It supports realistic-conflict experiments, social-identity experiments, minimal-group designs, threat manipulations, stereotype studies, contact interventions, superordinate-goal interventions, organizational rivalry research, political polarization studies, and institutional conflict-reduction designs.

## Core constructs

### Resource competition

Perception that two groups are competing for scarce material resources, opportunities, territory, jobs, funding, status, or institutional control.

### Zero-sum perception

Belief that one group can gain only if the other group loses.

### Identity salience

Degree to which group membership is active and psychologically important in a decision context.

### Group identification

Strength of attachment to, pride in, or emotional investment in one's group.

### Status threat

Perception that the group's social rank, recognition, legitimacy, influence, or dignity is declining.

### Realistic threat

Perception that the outgroup threatens material welfare, safety, resources, or political power.

### Symbolic threat

Perception that the outgroup threatens values, identity, meaning, religion, culture, morality, or social order.

### Stereotype endorsement

Degree to which generalized beliefs about the outgroup are accepted.

### Outgroup warmth and competence

Dimensions from stereotype-content research that capture perceived friendliness/trustworthiness and perceived capability/status.

### Intergroup anxiety

Discomfort, fear, or uncertainty about interacting with outgroup members.

### Perceived injustice

Belief that the ingroup has been unfairly treated, disrespected, deprived, or humiliated.

### Dehumanization

Perception of outgroup members as less fully human, moral, civilized, feeling, or deserving.

### Norm of retaliation

Perceived group expectation that members should respond to outgroup harm with punishment, revenge, or resistance.

### Contact quality

Quality of intergroup interaction, including trust, cooperation, equal status, non-hostile communication, and institutional support.

### Superordinate goal

A shared goal that cannot be achieved by either group alone and requires intergroup cooperation.

## Recommended designs

1. Realistic competition versus cooperative goal manipulation.
2. Minimal-group categorization study.
3. Status threat manipulation.
4. Symbolic threat manipulation.
5. Contact quality and equal-status intervention.
6. Superordinate-goal cooperative task.
7. Norm-of-retaliation salience manipulation.
8. Polarization discussion experiment.
9. Institutional legitimacy intervention.
10. Longitudinal escalation or de-escalation panel design.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish material competition from symbolic threat.
- Measure identity salience separately from group identification.
- Measure contact quantity and contact quality separately.
- Do not assume contact is beneficial without equal status, cooperation, common goals, and institutional support.
- Include perceived legitimacy and institutional trust.
- Measure dehumanization and exclusion support when studying severe conflict.
- Avoid treating conflict as symmetrical when power, domination, occupation, discrimination, or state violence are unequal.
- Include historical and institutional context when interpreting group hostility.
- Avoid pathologizing resistance by oppressed groups as mere bias.

## Ethics

Intergroup-conflict research can activate distress, prejudice, threat, identity threat, dehumanization, political hostility, or traumatic memory. Researchers should use careful debriefing, avoid inciting harm, protect vulnerable groups, avoid publishing identifiable group-targeting data, and distinguish analytical explanation from moral equivalence.
