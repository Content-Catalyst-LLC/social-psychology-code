# Research Protocol: Fundamental Attribution Error

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on the fundamental attribution error and correspondence bias: the tendency to over-infer disposition, character, attitude, or intention from behavior while underweighting situational constraints. It is designed for attribution experiments, constrained-choice vignette studies, observer judgment tasks, organizational accountability studies, legal/policy judgment research, cultural psychology, and institutional decision research.

## Core constructs

### Dispositional attribution

Degree to which behavior is explained by character, personality, attitude, intention, ability, or moral nature.

### Situational attribution

Degree to which behavior is explained by constraint, role pressure, incentives, norms, instructions, resources, threat, or environmental conditions.

### Perceived constraint

Degree to which the observer recognizes that the actor's behavior was constrained.

### Choice freedom

Degree to which the observer believes the actor had meaningful alternatives.

### Correspondence inference

Degree to which the observer infers that the behavior corresponds to the actor's true attitude, value, or disposition.

### Cognitive load

Degree to which the observer has limited processing capacity when making the judgment.

### Perspective taking

Whether the observer is prompted to consider the actor's constraints, role, incentives, and limited options.

### Accountability pressure

Whether the observer expects to justify the judgment with evidence.

### Evidence strength

Degree to which the available evidence supports dispositional versus situational explanation.

### Moral blame and punishment

Downstream consequences of attributional judgment.

## Recommended designs

1. Classic constrained-choice essay/attitude attribution experiment.
2. Low-choice versus high-choice vignette experiment.
3. Actor-observer asymmetry design.
4. Cognitive-load manipulation.
5. Perspective-taking correction condition.
6. Accountability-pressure manipulation.
7. Culture and self-construal comparison study.
8. Organizational blame attribution experiment.
9. Legal or policy vignette study.
10. Repeated institutional judgment simulation.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish dispositional attribution from moral blame.
- Measure perceived constraint and actual constraint separately.
- Include actor freedom or choice manipulation checks.
- Measure situational attribution directly rather than inferring it.
- Avoid treating every disposition inference as error.
- Include evidence strength when possible.
- Test correction conditions such as accountability, perspective taking, and constraint salience.
- Consider cultural models of personhood and agency.
- Analyze downstream consequences such as punishment, hiring, exclusion, or policy preference.

## Ethics

Research on attribution may involve judgments of blame, punishment, deviance, poverty, crime, disability, workplace failure, or marginalized social positions. Researchers should avoid designs that intensify stigma and should interpret dispositional attribution within structural and cultural context.
