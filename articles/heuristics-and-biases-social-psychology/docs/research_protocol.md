# Research Protocol: Heuristics and Biases

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on judgment under uncertainty, heuristics, cognitive biases, bounded rationality, anchoring, availability, representativeness, affective judgment, framing effects, base-rate neglect, overconfidence, confirmation bias, loss aversion, calibration, social judgment, institutional decision-making, and debiasing interventions.

It supports laboratory experiments, online judgment tasks, vignette studies, numerical estimation tasks, legal and medical decision studies, organizational audits, public-policy experiments, risk-perception studies, and simulations of institutional bias accumulation.

## Core constructs

### Heuristic

A simplified strategy or rule for making judgments under uncertainty, time pressure, incomplete information, or cognitive constraint.

### Bias

A systematic deviation between observed judgment and a normative benchmark, true value, calibrated estimate, or more complete model.

### Anchoring

Insufficient adjustment away from an initial value or reference point.

### Availability

Judgment based on the ease with which examples, memories, or narratives come to mind.

### Representativeness

Judgment based on similarity to a prototype, often at the expense of base-rate information.

### Affect heuristic

Judgment guided by immediate positive or negative feeling toward a target, event, policy, or outcome.

### Framing effect

A shift in judgment or choice caused by the presentation of equivalent information in different frames.

### Overconfidence

A mismatch between subjective confidence and actual accuracy.

### Confirmation bias

The tendency to seek, interpret, or weight evidence in ways that favor prior beliefs or expectations.

### Debiasing

A procedure designed to reduce systematic error, such as base-rate prompts, accountability, structured comparison, calibration feedback, slowing procedures, or reference-class forecasting.

## Recommended designs

1. Anchoring experiment with low, neutral, and high anchors.
2. Base-rate neglect experiment using representativeness cues.
3. Availability manipulation using vivid, recent, repeated, or emotionally salient examples.
4. Affect-risk-benefit judgment task.
5. Gain/loss framing experiment.
6. Confidence-calibration task with objective accuracy.
7. Confirmation-bias evidence-selection task.
8. Debiasing intervention with base-rate prompts or accountability.
9. Institutional decision-quality simulation.
10. Mixed-method design combining quantitative estimates and qualitative reasoning traces.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish random error from systematic bias.
- Specify the normative benchmark.
- Measure true value or reference-class probability when possible.
- Measure confidence and accuracy separately.
- Distinguish judgment error from preference or value disagreement.
- Avoid treating all heuristics as irrational.
- Account for ecological fit: a heuristic can be adaptive in one environment and misleading in another.
- Record response time where effort or automaticity is theoretically relevant.
- In institutional contexts, measure incentives, accountability, workflow constraints, and feedback quality.
- Do not use bias language to shame participants; treat shortcuts as ordinary features of bounded cognition.

## Ethics

Heuristics-and-biases research often studies error in judgment. Researchers should avoid humiliating participants, overclaiming irrationality, or presenting bias as a personal defect. In institutional settings, findings should be used to improve systems, feedback, accountability, and decision design rather than merely blaming individual decision makers.
