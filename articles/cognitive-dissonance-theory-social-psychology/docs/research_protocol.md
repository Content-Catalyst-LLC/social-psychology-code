# Research Protocol: Cognitive Dissonance Theory

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on cognitive dissonance, attitude change, belief justification, forced compliance, post-decision dissonance, effort justification, belief disconfirmation, self-affirmation, identity threat, action-based conflict, moral rationalization, and institutional commitment escalation.

It supports laboratory experiments, vignette studies, free-choice paradigms, counter-attitudinal advocacy studies, effort-justification studies, belief-disconfirmation studies, political or ideological commitment studies, organizational escalation studies, and simulations of institutional inertia.

## Core constructs

### Cognitive dissonance

An aversive psychological state produced by inconsistency among cognitions, attitudes, values, behaviors, commitments, or identity-relevant beliefs.

### Consonant cognition

A cognition that supports or fits with another cognition.

### Dissonant cognition

A cognition that conflicts with another cognition.

### Perceived choice

The extent to which a participant believes they freely chose the dissonance-producing behavior.

### Perceived responsibility

The extent to which a participant feels responsible for the behavior or consequence producing dissonance.

### Identity threat

The extent to which inconsistency threatens self-concept, moral identity, competence, rationality, or group identity.

### Self-affirmation

A procedure or process through which people restore self-integrity by affirming valued aspects of the self.

### Spreading of alternatives

The post-decision tendency to increase evaluation of a chosen option and decrease evaluation of a rejected option.

### Effort justification

The tendency to increase the value of an outcome after costly effort, sacrifice, or initiation.

### Belief disconfirmation

Exposure to evidence that contradicts a valued belief, prophecy, ideology, policy, or commitment.

### Institutional rationalization

Organizational or institutional reinterpretation of evidence to preserve legitimacy, identity, or commitment.

## Recommended designs

1. Forced-compliance experiment with high and low perceived choice.
2. Free-choice paradigm with pre-choice and post-choice option ratings.
3. Effort-justification paradigm with low, moderate, and severe effort costs.
4. Belief-disconfirmation study with measures of commitment and proselytizing.
5. Self-affirmation experiment before or after dissonance induction.
6. Identity-threat manipulation using moral, professional, political, or group-relevant inconsistency.
7. Public-versus-private commitment design.
8. Institutional escalation simulation with sunk cost and evidence strength.
9. Action-readiness study measuring response latency and conflict.
10. Mixed-method design combining quantitative attitude change and qualitative justification coding.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish attitude change from public compliance.
- Measure perceived choice and responsibility, not only behavior.
- Measure identity threat and self-relevance.
- Separate dissonance reduction from impression management.
- Include self-perception alternatives when attitudes are weak or ambiguous.
- Measure post-decision revaluation before and after commitment.
- Avoid inducing excessive shame or moral threat.
- In institutional contexts, distinguish psychological rationalization from material incentives and political constraints.
- Preregister exclusion rules and scoring procedures where possible.

## Ethics

Dissonance studies can induce discomfort, guilt, embarrassment, self-threat, or identity threat. Researchers should minimize harm, debrief carefully, avoid humiliating participants, and make clear that dissonance is an ordinary psychological process rather than a moral failure.
