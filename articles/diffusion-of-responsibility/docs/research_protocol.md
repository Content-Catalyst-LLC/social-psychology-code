# Research Protocol: Diffusion of Responsibility

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on diffusion of responsibility as a psychological and institutional process through which individual obligation weakens when responsibility appears shared. It is designed for bystander-intervention experiments, helping-behavior studies, emergency-response simulations, organizational accountability studies, reporting systems, institutional-risk analysis, and public-safety research.

## Core constructs

### Bystander count

Number of perceived potential responders.

### Ambiguity level

Degree to which the situation is unclear, uncertain, or open to multiple interpretations.

### Private concern

Participant's private sense that something is wrong or intervention may be needed.

### Perceived group concern

Participant's inference about how concerned others are.

### Pluralistic ignorance gap

Private concern minus perceived group concern. A positive gap indicates that the participant is privately concerned while perceiving the group as less concerned.

### Evaluation apprehension

Fear of embarrassment, social judgment, overreacting, or intervening incorrectly.

### Perceived personal responsibility

Subjective sense that the participant personally must act.

### Role clarity

Degree to which responsibilities and response expectations are explicit.

### Intervention efficacy

Perceived ability to help safely and effectively.

### Social visibility

Degree to which action or inaction is visible to others.

### Leadership cue

Signal from a leader or authority indicating that action is expected.

### Accountability assignment

Degree to which responsibility is assigned to a specific person or role.

### Organizational fragmentation

Degree to which a responsibility is split across units, departments, committees, tools, vendors, or roles.

### Intervention decision

Whether the participant helps, reports, escalates, or takes responsibility.

## Recommended designs

1. Classic bystander-count emergency experiment.
2. Ambiguity and pluralistic-ignorance manipulation.
3. Evaluation-apprehension condition.
4. Role-clarity intervention study.
5. Direct-appeal versus general-call experiment.
6. Emergency-response training study.
7. Organizational reporting experiment.
8. Accountability-fragmentation vignette study.
9. Leadership-cue intervention study.
10. Longitudinal institutional nonresponse simulation.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish bystander effect from diffusion of responsibility.
- Measure perceived personal responsibility directly.
- Measure private concern and perceived group concern separately.
- Include ambiguity and evaluation apprehension.
- Include role clarity and intervention efficacy.
- Distinguish failure to notice, failure to interpret, failure to assume responsibility, and failure to act.
- In organizational research, measure accountability structure rather than only individual motivation.
- Avoid treating all collective inaction as individual apathy.

## Ethics

Emergency and harm scenarios can be distressing. Use debriefing, avoid unnecessary trauma exposure, protect participants from shame, and interpret nonintervention in relation to social context, role ambiguity, uncertainty, and institutional structure.
