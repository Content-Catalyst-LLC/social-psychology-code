# Research Protocol: Attribution Theory

## Purpose

This protocol supports empirical and simulated research on attribution theory, causal explanation, internal and external attribution, correspondent inference, covariation reasoning, responsibility judgment, attribution bias, hostile attribution bias, achievement motivation, cultural variation, and institutional blame.

It supports laboratory experiments, vignette studies, educational studies, legal responsibility studies, organizational review studies, conflict and aggression research, cross-cultural attribution research, public-policy framing studies, and simulations of institutional blame.

## Core constructs

### Attribution

An inferred causal explanation for behavior, outcome, success, failure, harm, or social event.

### Dispositional attribution

Causal explanation emphasizing actor characteristics such as ability, motive, intention, effort, personality, morality, or stable traits.

### Situational attribution

Causal explanation emphasizing external context such as role pressure, constraint, coercion, institutional rules, task difficulty, opportunity, environment, or social pressure.

### Consensus

Whether other people behave similarly in the same situation.

### Distinctiveness

Whether the actor behaves similarly across many situations or only in this specific situation.

### Consistency

Whether the actor repeatedly behaves this way in the same situation over time.

### Stability

Whether the attributed cause is stable or temporary.

### Controllability

Whether the attributed cause is under personal control.

### Responsibility judgment

Inference about whether the actor should be held morally, legally, institutionally, or socially accountable.

### Correspondence bias

Tendency to infer stable dispositions from behavior even when situational factors explain that behavior.

### Hostile attribution bias

Tendency to infer hostile intent from ambiguous behavior.

### Attributional complexity

Degree to which a person uses multiple causes, context, and interactional explanations rather than simple single-cause accounts.

## Recommended designs

1. Ambiguous-behavior vignette experiment.
2. Actor-observer perspective study.
3. Success/failure achievement attribution task.
4. Consensus-distinctiveness-consistency covariation task.
5. Legal responsibility and blame judgment vignette.
6. Hostile attribution bias task.
7. Organizational performance review simulation.
8. Cross-cultural agency and situational constraint study.
9. Attributional complexity and intergroup judgment study.
10. Institutional blame simulation with audit and accountability conditions.

## Measurement cautions

- Distinguish causal attribution from moral evaluation.
- Measure internal and external attribution separately rather than assuming they are strict opposites.
- Measure controllability separately from internality.
- Measure stability separately from dispositionality.
- Measure intentionality, choice, and foreseeability when studying responsibility.
- Include situational-constraint information explicitly.
- Avoid treating culture as a simple individualism/collectivism binary.
- Separate hostile intent inference from negative affect.
- In institutional contexts, distinguish individual blame from system-level causation.
- Preregister responsibility and blame outcomes when studying high-stakes moral judgment.

## Ethics

Attribution research often involves blame, responsibility, failure, stigma, aggression, poverty, disability, illness, conflict, punishment, and social harm. Researchers should avoid reinforcing stereotypes, stigmatizing communities, or presenting structural disadvantage as individual deficiency.
